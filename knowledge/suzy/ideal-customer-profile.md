# Suzy Ideal Customer Profile — Proactive Marketing Decision-Maker

**Source:** ICP Deep Dive (imported 2026-04-21)

---

## ICP Narrative (Foundational)

Meet our new Ideal Customer Profile (ICP): a proactive marketing decision-maker at a large brand. This is not a traditional market researcher tucked away crunching surveys — it's the marketing leader on the front lines, responsible for driving growth and crafting strategy. Picture a **Brand Director or Marketing VP at a Fortune 1000 consumer brand** (think CPG, food & bev, finance, tech), or a **Head of Marketing at an upper mid-market firm**. They arrive at work with mounting pressure from the C-suite to deliver measurable results — every campaign must prove ROI. Their emotional state is a mix of urgency and accountability. On one hand, they're excited by creative opportunities to build the brand; on the other, they feel the weight of quarterly targets and "what have you done for me lately" expectations. They know their success or failure is highly visible. A major product launch or campaign flop could make or break their reputation internally, so fear of missing signals or making a bad call looms constantly. As one marketing manager put it, *"Errors in judgment are often a result of incorrect assumptions regarding the customer's requirements"* — our ICP is determined not to let that happen to them.

### Pressures & Incentives

This marketer is accountable for tangible growth metrics (sales lift, market share, brand equity scores) and often has compensation tied to performance. They get kudos for bold wins (a campaign that goes viral, a successful product launch) — these are career-making moves. Conversely, safe, reactive behavior might keep things steady but won't get them promoted. They feel an incentive to be proactive and visionary, yet the company culture may tacitly encourage caution (nobody wants to be the scapegoat for an experiment gone wrong). Politically, they navigate a web of stakeholders: the sales VP wants more leads, the CFO demands justification for budget increases, the CEO is asking why the brand isn't trending on TikTok yet. Our ICP is sandwiched between high-level strategic demands and on-the-ground execution realities, which creates daily tension. They must speak the language of business ("here's the projected ROI of this campaign") while staying creative and consumer-focused.

### Motivations & Fears

Deep down, this marketing leader thrives on making an impact. They chose this career to shape brands that matter in consumers' lives. They light up when they see a campaign idea resonating with the public or when data reveals an insight that could unlock growth. They want to be proactive — to set trends rather than chase them. They envision themselves as strategic drivers of the business, not order-takers. A big part of their motivation is also personal career growth: being known as the marketer who led a successful rebrand or doubled a product line's sales is a badge of honor.

On the flip side, they fear stagnation and irrelevance. One unspoken fear is becoming "just an executor" who does as they're told and never influences strategy. They also fear making decisions based on faulty information — nothing is worse than confidently pursuing a strategy only to find out later the consumer didn't want it. In fact, lack of understanding the customer or misjudging the market are among the most cited reasons for marketing decision failures. Our ICP hates the idea of a blind spot. They worry: *"Am I truly in touch with what our customer wants right now? Or am I flying on assumptions?"*

### Daily Reality and Emotional Context

On a typical day, this marketing decision-maker's inbox and meeting calendar are overflowing with inputs. At 9 AM, they have an internal sales update meeting where they learn one product line is underperforming in the Midwest — the sales team asks "Can marketing do something?" Right after, they jump on a call with an agency about the new campaign creative, which the Legal team has red-lined for being off-brand. Meanwhile, Slack is blowing up with a viral news trend that might relate to their brand — should they respond? In between, they get a dashboard alert that a competitor launched a new feature, and social media sentiment is surging for the competitor's brand.

Information is coming at them from all sides: research reports, social listening feeds, performance analytics, sales figures, customer service call logs. It's a firehose of "messy, fragmented customer signals", scattered across different systems. This person is **drowning in data yet thirsty for insight**. A major frustration is that despite having so much data, it's not synthesized into a clear story — there's no single, current picture of the customer that they trust. They constantly worry they might miss a critical signal in the noise.

Time is never on their side. If a question arises on Monday ("Why are Gen-Z signups down this month?"), the expectation is to have a hypothesis and action by end of week, if not end of day. But getting a reliable answer fast is hard: commissioning a formal study via the Insights team might take weeks — time they simply don't have. Indeed, many marketers today struggle to access insights quickly enough to make impactful decisions; waiting 6–12 months for a traditional analysis is not feasible when decisions are needed now. Our ICP lives this reality — they've sat through meetings where stakeholders roll their eyes at research timelines ("six weeks for a survey? We need to decide in six days"). This constant time crunch leaves them feeling anxious and often forces them to choose between speed and certainty. They crave a faster way to go from question to answer, without sacrificing too much confidence. As it stands, they often make imperfect decisions under pressure, then brace for the Monday morning quarterbacking if results disappoint.

Emotionally, this world oscillates between exhilaration and stress. The proactive marketer finds joy in spotting an emerging consumer trend and rallying the team to capitalize on it — that is the proactive rush they live for. But they also feel isolation at times; if the organization's mindset is still "research-first and action-later," the ICP can feel like an outlier pushing others to move faster. They might privately lament how internal bureaucracy or skepticism slows down great ideas. ("Do we really need to double-check this with yet another focus group? We know what we need to do!") Every delay or hurdle adds to their stress, because they feel the market outside won't wait. When they do have a bold proposal, they sometimes encounter internal resistance or politics — e.g. a senior colleague questioning the data, or a committee diluting the creative vision. These setbacks are frustrating and emotionally draining; as one agency described, "marketing-by-committee" can strip the power and originality from a campaign, leaving the expert marketers feeling "frustrated" and second-guessed.

Our ICP yearns for **clarity and empowerment**: clear consumer signals, clear decisions, and the empowerment to act decisively. They want tools and a culture that let them stay in a proactive flow — identifying opportunities, making confident decisions, and seeing swift execution — rather than constantly fighting analysis paralysis, misalignment, or gut-driven detours. In short, they are a human ball of energy and intent, looking for any help they can get to cut through complexity and confidently drive their marketing vision forward.

## Decision-Making Model: From Signal to Action

How do marketing decisions actually flow from the first inkling of a "signal" to an executed action? In theory, it's a fairly linear process: **observe market signals → derive insights → decide strategy → take action → measure outcome**. In practice, this process is messy and fraught with delays. Let's break down the typical decision journey for our ICP and highlight where things break, slow down, or get distorted.

### 1. Signal Emergence (Observe)

A "signal" can be any notable input: a change in sales data, a new consumer trend, a competitor move, a viral tweet about the brand — anything that might call for a marketing decision. For example, say the signal is a sudden drop in app sign-ups among Gen-Z users, or an uptick in Google searches for a certain product feature.

**Problems at this stage: Signal overload.** These marketers are bombarded with data points from disparate sources — research reports, social listening, web analytics, retail sales, etc. — with no coherent, real-time picture of what's happening. Important signals can be lost in noise. Conversely, teams might fixate on loud but misleading signals (e.g. one vocal customer complaint that isn't actually representative). The fragmentation means our ICP often doesn't trust that they're seeing the full story. One marketing leader might get a social media alert and feel urgency, while another stakeholder has a report saying the opposite — leading to confusion. Internal politics can also start here: if a signal challenges the status quo, some may ignore or downplay inconvenient information, slowing acknowledgement of the issue.

### 2. Insight Generation (Interpret)

Once a signal is recognized, the next step is making sense of it — answering "why is this happening?" and "what does it mean we should do?" In our Gen-Z sign-up drop example, this might involve digging into data or commissioning quick research: maybe a survey or some focus groups to understand attitudes.

**Problems at this stage: Slow synthesis and analysis bottlenecks.** Teams often spend weeks (or even months) translating raw inputs into "what really matters", by which time the moment may have passed. Traditional research cycles are lengthy — scripting a survey, fielding it, analyzing results — and time pressure is the enemy of thorough analysis. In fact, about two-thirds of marketing managers say lack of time regularly hampers decision analysis, forcing decisions "under uncertainty". Under such pressure, they may rely on partial data or gut feel. This is where distortion can creep in: confirmation bias (seeing what you expect in the data) or groupthink in interpretation meetings. Additionally, if they do involve an Insights specialist or vendor, internal coordination can slow things down. Multiple iterations ("We need more data on X…") can drag out the insight phase until it collides with deadlines. Our ICP often faces a cruel irony: they have more data than ever, but not enough time or clarity to extract insight, leading to what one observer called "analysis paralysis".

### 3. Alignment & Decision (Decide)

Armed with some insight (or at least a hypothesis), the marketer now needs to make a decision and get buy-in. This could be deciding a strategy or action — e.g. "Let's target Gen-Z with a new message focused on value pricing, and increase our TikTok spend". In a simpler world, the marketing lead could just decide and move on it. In reality, stakeholder alignment is often a gating factor. The plan might need approval from the CMO, or at least consensus across the brand team, digital team, and maybe legal or finance if budgets are shifting.

**Problems at this stage: Stakeholder drag and politics.** In many organizations (especially enterprises), decisions get kicked to cross-functional meetings or committees. Each stakeholder may have their own viewpoint or agenda — e.g. the Insights manager questions the research validity ("Is this data trustworthy?"), the sales director pushes for a different angle, etc. "Marketing-by-committee" can water down bold ideas and slow decisions to a crawl. Our ICP might find that by the time everyone agrees, the original insight has staled or the creative idea has been diluted to something "safe." Another issue is low confidence and credibility: if the insight or recommendation isn't bulletproof, it will be contested — "Are we sure about this? The methodology could be flawed". In some cases, the highest-paid person's opinion (HiPPO effect) can override the data entirely, distorting the decision to align with politics rather than consumer evidence. This stage is rife with emotional undercurrents: fear of risk can lead to reactive, least-common-denominator decisions (choosing an option that offends no one, but also excites no one). The proactive marketer tries to counter this with data and persuasive storytelling, but it's an uphill battle if tools don't provide clear, credible insights to instill confidence.

### 4. Execution (Act)

Once a decision is made — say, a new campaign concept is approved — the action moves to execution. The team must translate the decision into a concrete campaign or initiative: briefing creative teams or an agency, setting up targeting in ad platforms, adjusting the website or product, etc.

**Problems at this stage: Operational gaps and friction.** Even when everyone agrees on what to do, turning that decision into real campaigns can be surprisingly manual and siloed. Our ICP might have to coordinate across multiple tools and teams: one team pulls a list of Gen-Z customers from the CRM, another team builds new creative assets, another adjusts the e-commerce site. There's often no single system linking the insight to the action. This manual orchestration is error-prone and slow. For example, if the decision was to target a new segment, the marketer might have to export survey data, import it into a customer database, then define that audience in an ad platform — a process riddled with chances for mismatch or delay. Furthermore, if the decision requires content creation (new messaging), it can get bottlenecked in review cycles (legal approval, brand compliance checks, etc.). In an enterprise especially, governance and brand safety checks are necessary but can slow the deployment of a decision into market. Our ICP often feels frustration here: they finally got a decision made, but now execution is taking weeks. Every extra day feels costly when they're trying to act on a timely insight.

### 5. Measurement & Feedback (Adjust)

After execution, a truly proactive decision loop would measure results and feed learnings back into the next cycle. The marketer will look at campaign performance, sales outcomes, brand metrics, etc., to evaluate if the decision achieved the desired outcome (did Gen-Z signups improve? Did the new messaging move the needle?).

**Problems at this stage: Disconnected learning and proving ROI.** In many orgs, the tools that execute campaigns (ad platforms, CRM, etc.) are not seamlessly connected to the earlier insight. Closing the loop — tying outcomes back to the original decision — often requires yet another round of data wrangling. If results are positive, the marketer must still prove it in terms the CFO understands (e.g. "This decision drove a 10% lift in sign-ups, which is worth $X"). If results are negative or mixed, there's the challenge of diagnosing why. Without an integrated loop, teams resort to ad-hoc analysis and meetings to interpret results, which slows organizational learning. Worse, if results are poor, retrospective politics can kick in ("I never fully supported that idea…"), eroding trust. Many marketers lament how hard it is to consistently learn from each campaign — the data might exist but gets analyzed in silo, so the next project might repeat mistakes.

### Summary: Major Breakdowns in the Decision Flow

- **Information overload and fragmentation** at the start make it hard to see the signal through the noise.
- **Time pressure and resource constraints** make thorough analysis difficult, resulting in slower or less-informed insight generation.
- **Internal misalignment and risk aversion** can distort or delay the decision, as multiple stakeholders and doubts creep in.
- **Siloed execution tools and processes** create an operational gap, slowing action and introducing error.
- **Lack of integrated feedback loops** means even after action, it's hard to rapidly optimize or confidently attribute success/failure back to decisions.

Our ICP constantly lives at the intersection of these failure points. They recognize these issues: for instance, they know "lack of understanding of the consumer" or "misjudging customer needs" leads to bad outcomes, and they desperately want to avoid those fates. They need a decision-making flow that is faster, clearer, and more connected end-to-end than what they have today. Right now, that "chasm between signal and action" is the bane of their existence.

## Proactive vs. Reactive Framework

Not all marketing decision-makers behave alike. A key insight for Suzy is the distinction between **proactive marketers** and **reactive marketers** (or as the prompt puts it, proactive decision-makers vs reactive order-takers).

### Mindset

| | Proactive Marketer | Reactive Marketer |
|---|---|---|
| **Self-perception** | Owner of outcomes and strategist | Executor of tasks, responder to requests |
| **Core question** | "How can I get in front of the next challenge or opportunity?" | "Tell me what needs to be done, and I'll do it." |
| **Comfort with ambiguity** | Comfortable; takes initiative | Prefers clear instructions and known procedures |
| **Stance toward change** | Growth mindset — new consumer behaviors are opportunities | Risk-averse — prefers the status quo |
| **Default instinct** | Seize opportunities | Avoid mistakes |

### Behaviors

**Proactive decision-makers initiate action.** They don't wait for a formal research request — if they notice a potential issue or idea, they'll spin up a quick survey or dive into analytics on their own. They bring new insights to meetings: *"I saw early signals that our competitor's eco-friendly packaging is trending; I went ahead and polled some consumers last night — here's what I found and here's what I propose we do."* They set the agenda by backing up ideas with data and pushing for strategic change. They seek out cross-functional collaboration preemptively. They go above and beyond their defined role because they're driven by outcomes, not just tasks.

**Reactive order-takers follow established processes.** They excel at executing clearly defined tasks and will reliably deliver what's asked — but only what's asked. If a superior says "we need a consumer survey on X," they'll do exactly that — but might not ask "What are we trying to decide from this?" They wait for approvals at each step, stick strictly within their lane, and often escalate decisions upward rather than take responsibility. One telling difference: proactive marketers prepare Plan B and Plan C; reactive marketers are caught off guard by anything outside the original plan.

### Environmental Factors

People are not purely proactive or reactive by birth — their environment heavily influences which mode dominates.

**Environments that foster proactivity:**
- Culture that rewards initiative and tolerates smart failures
- Leadership that visibly values new ideas and doesn't punish every failed experiment
- Emphasis on speed and agility as core values
- Access to enabling tools (self-service research, real-time dashboards)
- High-trust teams where members know each other's strengths

**Environments that create reactivity:**
- Strict hierarchies, high blame culture, or micromanagement
- Every decision requires approval from 3+ layers of management
- Conservative industries or legacy enterprises with "this is how we do things" mantras
- Past attempts at proactivity were ignored or shot down
- Siloed teams with low trust; "the noisiest person is the appointed leader"
- Data locked in an Insights department with formal approval required for every question

### Key Takeaway for Product Design

At every step, ask: *"Does this feature empower a marketer to act one step ahead, or does it only cater to someone reacting to a task?"* Catalyze proactive behaviors (initiative, insight-driven action, cross-team collaboration) and minimize the friction that keeps marketers in reactive mode.

## Upper Mid-Market vs. Enterprise Comparison

Our ICP exists in both upper mid-market and enterprise organizations, but the context of their organization's size and structure meaningfully affects their reality.

| Factor | Upper Mid-Market (~500–1,000 employees; growing company) | Enterprise (global brand, thousands of employees, multiple divisions) |
|---|---|---|
| **Decision Velocity** | **Higher velocity, faster turns.** Fewer layers of approval; the Head of Marketing or small leadership team can green-light campaigns on the spot. Reaction in days or weeks, not months. | **Lower velocity, longer cycles.** Multiple layers of stakeholders, formal processes. Major campaigns planned on annual cycles. Approval cycles of 3–9 months are common. |
| **Decision Complexity** | **Moderate; generalist roles.** One person handles brand strategy, analytics, and execution. Often no dedicated Insights team — the marketer is the insights team. Fewer internal stakeholders; one cross-functional meeting aligns key people. | **High; specialized roles.** Separate departments for Brand Management, Consumer Insights, Analytics, Regional Marketing, etc. A single decision may involve research reports, consulting analyses, regional market manager feedback — like a relay race with many baton passes. |
| **Stakeholders & Alignment** | **Small circle, high visibility.** Reports to CEO or small exec team. Cross-functional alignment = a quick Slack message. Decisions made informally. Each person carries more responsibility per decision. | **Large, formal committees and distributed teams.** "Brand steering committees" and cross-functional task forces. Global vs. local tensions (global brand manager wants consistency, local wants relevance). Trust is institutional — backed by evidence and reputable sources. |
| **Trust and Data Culture** | **Trust through relationships and quick proof.** Limited historical data; decisions lean on marketer's intuition + scrappy data. "Get it done" culture — a plausible insight from a credible person is enough. Resource-constrained, so wary of big bets that fail. | **Skeptical, validation-oriented trust.** Tons of historical reports, big data pipelines, experienced analysts. Every insight scrutinized: "Where did this come from? What's the methodology?" Often burned by "shiny new tools" before — pilot projects precede full trust. |
| **Scale and Complexity of Data** | **Less data silos, but also fewer data resources.** A handful of SaaS tools (Google Analytics, Shopify, basic CRM). May lack certain insights entirely (no consumer panel, no BI team). Rely on third-party or creative proxies. | **Many data sources, silo challenges.** Abundance of consumer data (brand tracking, CRM, social listening, third-party reports). Different sources often give different answers. Data lives in silos; marketers spend time manually integrating. |

### Product Design Implications

| Scenario | Implication for Suzy |
|---|---|
| Mid-market decision velocity | Product must deliver value quickly — real-time/immediate insights. Users move from question to decision in a single sitting. |
| Mid-market complexity | Emphasize breadth and integration — a one-stop platform covering many needs (survey, analytics, social listening). Simpler collaboration features suffice. |
| Mid-market stakeholders | Quick shareability — pull up a dashboard in a meeting with the CEO, send a concise summary to Sales head. |
| Mid-market data culture | Highly digestible, plainly explained insights. Small-scale, fast research (overnight results). |
| Mid-market data scale | Suzy can become their missing consumer panel. Easy integration with a handful of tools (Google Sheets, Slack). Simple setup — no IT department. |
| Enterprise velocity | Features that speed slow processes: executive-ready summaries, live shared dashboards, scenario planning. Track decisions and keep momentum over long cycles. |
| Enterprise complexity | Enable integration and handoffs. Insights packaged for downstream users (media planner, creative team). Permission controls and workspaces. |
| Enterprise stakeholders | Centralized knowledge and consensus-building. Single source of truth dashboard. Annotations and source visibility for Insights team trust. |
| Enterprise trust | Earn trust through transparency and reliability. Show sources, methodology, AI reasoning. Robust governance (permissions, approvals). |
| Enterprise data scale | Bridge data silos — combine survey insights with social sentiment and sales trends in one place. Handle large sample sizes, complex segmentations, enterprise BI integrations. |

### Summary of Differences

- **Mid-market:** Faster, leaner, trust-by-proximity. Needs a Swiss-army knife — quick, easy, broad coverage for a small team. Constrained by resources and time.
- **Enterprise:** Slower, more complex, scrutiny-heavy. Needs a specialized but integrative tool — transparency, collaboration, fits into a larger ecosystem. Constrained by coordination and complexity.

Both want better decisions. Product design must not assume one-size-fits-all — mid-market loves agility and simplicity while enterprise demands governance and depth. A modular design (core easy workflows plus advanced options/integrations) hits both segments. Global context is primarily an enterprise concern (regional teams, localization, multi-market rollout).

## Product Design Heuristics (Memorable Principles for R&D)

Five rules of thumb to guide daily decisions and trade-offs. Engineers and designers can use these as tiebreakers when in doubt.

### 1. "Speed Over Perfection" — Optimize for Decision Time, Not Research Depth

If our ICP faces a fast, good-enough insight now versus a perfect insight too late, they need speed. **If it's slow, it's broken.** Favor features that shorten the path from question to answer. Automate the busy-work (data cleaning, number crunching) to deliver insights in minutes or hours, not days. One-click summary reports and AI-generated insights are valuable — if they're quick. Be cautious about any feature that adds significant time, even if it offers analytical depth; make it optional, not default. The ICP will accept a directional insight with 90% confidence today over a 99% confidence insight next month. Offer deeper dives as add-ons, but the main flow must enable "speed to insight."

### 2. "Insight to Action, All in One Place" — Design for End-to-End Execution

Our ICP doesn't win by finding insights alone; they win by acting on them. Never leave the user at a dead end of "here's data, good luck." Every workflow should answer: *"And then what? How does this insight directly enable an action?"* **Integrate, don't isolate.** Examples:
- Turn a Gen-Z message winner into a target audience segment or a creative brief
- Export a consumer segment from a survey as an audience file for Facebook Ads
- Integrate with a CMS to feed top consumer quotes directly into content
- Track whether the user acted on an insight (mark decision as implemented)

Every insight should have a clear next step in the product — push to a colleague, spin up a quick clarification poll, start an "action brief." Serve the ICP's ultimate goal: **driving outcomes, not just learnings.**

### 3. "Clarity & Trust at a Glance" — Prioritize Transparency and Storytelling in the UX

Our ICP operates in high-pressure, cross-functional environments where they must quickly persuade others (and themselves) to trust the data. **No black boxes.** If AI analyzes something, show the reasoning (e.g. "Top reason: 68% of consumers said X"). Include source citations, confidence levels, drill-downs. Design for storytelling — the output should be something the ICP can take into a meeting where everyone immediately gets the point. Use visualizations annotated with the "so what." Provide one-click "generate deck slide" or "executive summary" outputs. The product should do some of the narrative building for the user. *Make insights obvious, understandable, and backed by evidence at a glance.*

### 4. "Meet Them Where They Work" — Integrate Seamlessly into Existing Workflows

Our ICP juggles many tools and spends their day in a few core platforms (Slack/Teams, project management, marketing dashboards, email). If Suzy sits siloed outside their daily flow, it risks being forgotten. **Go to the user, don't expect the user to come to you.** Invest in integrations:
- Deliver insights/notifications via Slack
- Auto-generate PowerPoint or Google Slides reports
- APIs and data connectors for BI tools and cloud storage
- Live polling or Q&A feature for in-meeting use
- Excel plugin / easy CSV export for Excel-heavy users

The more frictionlessly we fit into the existing workflow, the more indispensable the tool becomes. Don't create needless tool-switching.

### 5. "Guardrails, Not Roadblocks" — Empower Users While Enforcing Key Safeguards

Empower decision-makers to act quickly and confidently, but respect necessary guardrails (brand compliance, legal, data privacy). Too many restrictions = feels bureaucratic; too few = enterprise adoption falters. Build guardrails that guide and protect without stopping momentum:
- Inline warnings ("Sample size low, interpret with caution")
- Lightweight approval flows for big actions (push survey to 10,000 customers, auto-generate press release draft)
- Highlight off-brand wording and suggest alternatives (rather than blocking)
- Source attribution for AI outputs, content filters, audit trails

**Make the right way the easy way.** Ask: *"How might this be misused, and what guardrail can we add without ruining the UX?"* Empowerment with safety nets.

### Heuristics as Catchphrases

1. **Make it fast** (Speed over perfection)
2. **Design for action** (Insight to action, all in one place)
3. **Show your work** (Clarity & trust at a glance)
4. **Be everywhere they are** (Meet them where they work)
5. **Protect without constraining** (Guardrails, not roadblocks)

## Implications for Suzy

Embracing this ICP reframes Suzy's mission and priorities. **Suzy must prioritize being a decision enablement platform over a traditional research tool.**

### From Insights to Outcomes

Measure Suzy's success not by the number of surveys completed, but by the number of decisions and actions it informs. A fundamental pivot from online research tool to **marketing decision platform**. Per internal strategy: *"being a best-in-class online survey platform is no longer enough. We need to evolve beyond delivering insights to automating actions that drive business outcomes."* Prioritize features that tie into outcome generation (integrations, action-oriented outputs). If a feature doesn't help a team make a decision faster or execute a campaign better, question why we're doing it. *Do not* optimize for academic or exploratory research use cases untied to decisions. Invest instead in AI summarization, scenario modeling, and playbooks for common decisions.

### Target User and Use Cases Expansion

Focus on marketing decision-makers (brand managers, marketing directors) — expanding beyond the insights department buyer. Internal strategy: *"we presently serve insights teams, but need to capture wallet share with teams outside of Market Research."* This means:
- Rethink UI and terminology to be marketing-friendly ("audience" not "panel"; "campaign feedback" not "survey respondents")
- Template the product around marketing workflows: "Ad Concept Tester", "Consumer Trend Radar", "Brand Health Monitor"
- Avoid research jargon and complex methodology settings in the main flow
- Advanced research controls (sampling menus, weighting schemes) should be under the hood or in advanced mode

### Speed and Agility as a North Star

Live up to the speed expectations of proactive marketers. Invest in tech that delivers results in hours or days: expanded on-demand audience, AI-analyzed open-ends in real time, always-on dashboards, real-time capabilities. Modern marketing teams value *"speed, measurable ROI, and tools that turn data into action at scale."* Suzy must be the tool you open when something happens now and you need an answer by end of day. Stay agile to new trends — TikTok polling/sentiment, emerging data sources — and integrate them quickly.

### Integration into Marketing Ecosystem

Suzy should be a connected node in the martech stack. Strengthen integrations with Tableau, Slack, Marketo, Salesforce, project management suites. Remove any adoption barrier. Enterprise: single sign-on, compliance, security standards. Don't think of Suzy as an isolated survey app — it's part of the marketing decision ecosystem, feeding data in and out. The more ingrained in daily workflows, the harder to displace.

Pricing implication: Prefer subscription or usage-based models that encourage many quick inquiries — not expensive per-study costs that dissuade use. We want *consistent daily usage* by marketers making decisions, not one-off project-based usage.

### Explicit Non-Goals

- **Not** the #1 tool for academic research or elaborate, long-term market research untied to immediate decisions
- **Not** supporting 6-month conjoint analyses with dozens of segments and 100-page reports (old paradigm)
- **Not** catering heavily to pure data scientist/statistician personas if it complicates marketer UX
- **Not** optimizing for research ops use cases (panel management, fine sampling control) at the expense of simplicity
- **Not** focusing on one-off data dumps or selling raw data — the ICP wants answers and guidance, not weeks of data to analyze themselves

Retrain sales/CS to sell **outcomes** ("this will help your marketing team decide and act faster"), not technical specs.

### Reframing Success Stories and Metrics

Collect and highlight success stories in decision/outcome terms:
> *"Brand X used Suzy to rapidly identify a new consumer trend and launch a product in 1/3 the time, resulting in +15% sales."*

Case studies and product marketing should feature **decisions and actions taken** using Suzy — not just "insights found." Build features that let users close the loop and input outcomes (concept test → launch → outcome tracking). This gives users value (seeing decision → result) and gives us data to prove aggregate ROI.

### The Mantra

Suzy's positioning should become: *"Know what your customer wants and do something about it — all before your competition even figures it out."*

Shifting to serve the proactive marketing decision-maker means Suzy must embody **agility, clarity, and action-orientation** in every aspect. A pivot from behind-the-scenes insights generator to **front-and-center decision compass for marketing teams**. Focus on moving the user *"from confusion to clarity to action"* quickly — and Suzy differentiates itself from commoditized research tools.
